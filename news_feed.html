This low-cost catalyst helps turn seawater into fuel at scale - University of Rochester
2020-07-15 - General News (Google News)
Researchers at the University of Rochester, in collaboration with the Naval Research Laboratory, the University of Pittsburgh, and OxEon Energy, have developed a potassium-promoted molybdenum carbide catalyst that efficiently converts carbon dioxide to carbon monoxide, an essential step in transforming seawater into fuel. This breakthrough, described in a paper in the journal Energy & Environmental Science, marks the first demonstration that such a catalyst can be used on an industrial scale. The team conducted extensive experiments at molecular, laboratory, and pilot scales to confirm the catalyst's effectiveness and suitability for large-scale applications, potentially enabling Navy ships to generate their own fuel from seawater.

Professor Porosoff wins award to develop reverse water-gas shift catalysts
2018-01-01 - UR Chem Eng News (2018)
Assistant Professor Marc D. Porosoff of the University of Rochester's chemical engineering department has received a $60,000 University Research Award to develop a new technique for creating nano-sized reverse water-gas shift (RWGS) catalysts. This research aims to facilitate the hydrogenation of CO2 into useful products such as plastics, chemicals, and fuels. Additionally, the project could benefit from collaboration with the Advanced Materials for Powerful Lasers (AMPL) center, which focuses on using laser-based materials processing to develop nano-catalysts for CO2 hydrogenation.
